Scope expansion of cobalt-catalyzed C-H bond activation for 3-component synthesis

The Ellman lab had developed a cobalt-based catalyst designed to catalyze the sequential addition of two electrophilic coupling partners to a molecule's activated C-H bond. I expanded the scope of use for this catalyst, demonstrating its ability to stereoseletively catalyze the addition of dienes and aldehydes to benzamides. Three-component products from this catalyst's reactions provide straightforward access to desirable bioactive molecular structures, simplifing drug product synthesis.

Boerth, J. A., Maity, S., Williams, S. K., Mercado, B. Q. & Ellman, J. A. Selective and synergistic cobalt(iii)-catalysed three-component C–H bond addition to dienes and aldehydes. Nat Catal 1, 673–679 (2018).

Computational prediction of phosphoramidate prodrug binding to Hint1

Phosphoramidate caging is a strategy for sneaking phosphate-containing drugs across the cell membrane, increasing their permeability by neutralizing their negative charge. Once in cells, these caging groups are removed by a phosphoramidase that exists within the cell. Based on Hint1’s known phosphoramidase behavior on nucleotide substrates, we suspected it may be able to uncage other phosphoramidate-caged molecules. Using molecular docking, I provided computational predictions and associated statistical analysis describing the likely binding of a phosphoramidate-caged Pin1 inhibitor to the active site of Hint1, suggesting a mechanism for the uncaging and free inhibitor release we observed in cells. This work indicates an expanded substrate scope for Hint1, suggesting that the phosphoramidate prodrugging strategy can be used to increase the cell permeability of molecules other than the historical nucleotide-based drugs for which the strategy was designed.

Schwarz, D. M. C., Williams, S. K., Dillenburg, M., Wagner, C. R. & Gestwicki, J. E. A Phosphoramidate Strategy Enables Membrane Permeability of a Non-nucleotide Inhibitor of the Prolyl Isomerase Pin1. ACS Med. Chem. Lett. 11, 1704–1710 (2020).

Determining peptide structural requirements to activate the 20S proteasome

The proteasome is responsible for breaking down unneeded cellular proteins so that their component amino acids can be reused. It is activated in cells by partner proteins that stick their C termini into pockets on the proteasome surface. Peptides as short as six amino acids, containing the binding “HbYX” (hydrophobic-Tyrosine-any C terminus) motif, are capable of mimicking that behavior of activating the proteasome. As part of a broader investigation into the relationship between these peptides' structure and their activity, we synthesized and tested a peptide panel to better understand the requirements for proteasome activation by peptides. This study contributes to a structural understanding allostery in the proteasomal degradation pathway.

Opoku-Nsiah, K.A., de la Pena, A.H., Williams, S.K., Chopra, N., Sali, A., Lander, G.C., Gestwicki, J.E. The YΦ Motif Defines the Structure-Activity Relationships of Human 20S Proteasome Activators. Nat Commun, 13, 1226 (2022).
